Final-year dissertation · 2025–2026

An electro-thermal model of temperature rise
in Li-ion batteries during fast charging

Fast charging of lithium-ion batteries is widely sought after but accelerates aging through increased internal heat generation. This project builds a transparent, physics-based model that links charging rate to temperature rise and aging — without the complexity of a full electrochemical simulation.

The model treats the cell as a single thermal mass heated by resistive losses (I²R) and cooled by convection. The internal resistance follows an Arrhenius temperature dependence, making the governing ODE nonlinear. It was solved numerically in Python using the forward Euler method, with an analytical solution (constant I, constant R) used to verify convergence. Three aging metrics were then computed from the simulated temperature profile: peak temperature, time above a safe threshold, and SEI calendar capacity loss.
